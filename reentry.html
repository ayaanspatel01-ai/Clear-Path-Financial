For returning citizens

You served your time.
Now rebuild yours.

Returning from incarceration comes with real financial barriers. This page gives you honest, practical steps to navigate banking, housing, employment, and benefits.

ⓘ Laws and programs vary by state. The information here is general guidance. For help specific to your state, use the resources on our Resources page.

🏦 Banking access

Many banks reject people with a criminal record or negative banking history. Here's how to get an account open.

Look for "second chance" checking accounts

Banks like Chime, Wells Fargo (Opportunity Checking), and many credit unions offer accounts for people with past banking issues.

Avoid ChexSystems banks initially

Most big banks use ChexSystems to screen applicants. Second-chance accounts bypass this screening.

Get a prepaid debit card as a bridge

While you're building your banking history, a prepaid card lets you receive direct deposits and make purchases safely.

📈 Rebuilding credit from zero

If you have no credit or damaged credit, these steps will help you build a positive history over time.

Apply for a secured credit card

You deposit $200–$500 as collateral and get a card with that limit. Use it for small purchases and pay it off monthly.

Become an authorized user

If a family member or trusted friend has good credit, ask to be added to their card. Their history can help boost yours.

Get a credit-builder loan

Credit unions and apps like Self offer small loans designed specifically to build your credit score over 12–24 months.

💼 Employment & income gaps

A record doesn't have to mean unemployment. Know your rights and options.

Look for "ban the box" employers

Many companies have pledged not to ask about criminal history on initial applications. Search "fair chance employers" in your area.

Know your rights under state law

Many states limit what employers can ask about and when. Some offenses cannot legally be used against you after a certain number of years.

Consider self-employment or trade work

Skilled trades, freelancing, and gig work often have fewer background check barriers and can provide immediate income.

🏠 Finding housing

Private landlords can be more flexible than large property management companies. Here's how to navigate the search.

Target individual landlords

Small private landlords often make case-by-case decisions. Be upfront, bring references, and offer extra deposit if possible.

Look for reentry housing programs

Many cities have transitional housing and reentry housing programs specifically for returning citizens. Contact your local reentry organization.

Get a reference letter

A letter from a parole officer, counselor, employer, or pastor can go a long way in convincing a landlord to give you a chance.

🏛️ Benefits & government programs

You may qualify for government assistance depending on your offense type and state.

SNAP food benefits

Drug felony convictions no longer automatically disqualify you from SNAP in most states. Check your state's specific rules.

Medicaid & healthcare

You may qualify for Medicaid upon release. Apply immediately through healthcare.gov or your state's Medicaid office.

Pell Grants for education

Most people with criminal records can now receive federal Pell Grants for college. Education is one of the strongest paths to financial stability.
