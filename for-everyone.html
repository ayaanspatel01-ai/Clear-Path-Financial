Financial basics

Money skills for real life

No matter your starting point, these practical tips will help you take control of your money and build toward something better.

Budgeting basics

A budget isn't about restriction — it's about knowing where your money goes so you can make intentional choices.

  • Track every dollar for 30 daysWrite down or use a free app to track every purchase. Awareness is the first step.
  • Use the 50/30/20 rule50% on needs, 30% on wants, 20% on savings or debt payoff. Adjust based on your income.
  • Pay yourself firstBefore spending on anything, set aside even $10–$20 into savings. Small amounts build habits.
  • Cut subscriptions you forgot aboutCheck your bank statement for recurring charges. Cancel what you don't use.

Understanding credit

Your credit score affects your ability to rent a home, get a job, and borrow money. Here's how to improve it.

  • Check your credit report for freeGo to AnnualCreditReport.com — you're entitled to a free report from all three bureaus every year.
  • Pay on time, every timePayment history is 35% of your score. Even minimum payments protect your score.
  • Keep balances lowTry to use less than 30% of your available credit limit on each card.
  • Don't open too many accounts at onceEach application creates a hard inquiry which can temporarily lower your score.

Saving on a tight income

You don't need a high income to start saving. Small consistent habits add up faster than you think.

  • Start with $5 a weekThat's $260 a year. It's not about the amount — it's about the habit.
  • Use a free savings accountLook for accounts with no minimum balance and no fees. Online banks like Chime are good options.
  • Save windfalls automaticallyTax refund? Birthday money? Put at least half into savings before spending any of it.
  • Build a $500 emergency fund firstThis is your safety net. Before paying extra on debt, save $500 for unexpected expenses.

Avoiding scams & predatory lending

Payday loans, rent-to-own stores, and financial scams target people in vulnerable situations. Know the warning signs.

  • Avoid payday loansThey charge 300–400% interest annually. A $300 loan can turn into $1,000+ of debt quickly.
  • If it sounds too good, it isAny offer promising fast cash, debt elimination, or guaranteed approval is almost always a scam.
  • Never pay upfront for a loanLegitimate lenders do not charge fees before giving you money.
  • Look for nonprofit credit counselorsThe NFCC (nfcc.org) connects you with free, certified credit counselors.
